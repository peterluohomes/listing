Every active listing pursues three goals at once: sell the property, maximize seller satisfaction, and build the agent's reputation. They sound coordinate, but they are not. They exist in a hierarchy, and respecting that hierarchy is what separates marketing that produces results from activity that merely looks like marketing. This document is the runnable procedure for every active listing — phase by phase, week by week — designed so the work that genuinely moves the property is also the work the seller can see.
Part One

The Three Goals — and Why Their Hierarchy Matters

The most important strategic decision of any listing is which goal comes first

Most agents say they want to do all three of the following equally well:

  • Sell the property — at the right price, in a reasonable time
  • Maximize seller satisfaction — leave the seller feeling well-served
  • Build the agent's reputation — generate referrals, future listings, recognition

But pursuing all three with equal weight is what produces scattered effort. Tactics that partially serve all three but fully serve none. Marketing that looks impressive but doesn't produce buyers. Activities that feel client-serving but are really self-promotion. Self-promotion wrapped in client-service language.

The honest truth is that these three goals exist in a strict hierarchy, and getting that hierarchy right is what makes a great agent different from an average one.

The Hierarchy

1. Sell the property — paramount

Every other goal is downstream of this one. If the property doesn't sell, or sells slowly, or sells below its potential, nothing else matters. The seller wasn't satisfied. The agent's reputation wasn't promoted — it was damaged. The promotional benefits of tactics that didn't actually help sell the home are negative, not zero.

2. Seller satisfaction — built almost entirely from Goal 1

Worth internalizing deeply: seller satisfaction is overwhelmingly determined by whether the property sold well, and only marginally by anything else. All the gestures, the weekly updates, the personal touches — they matter, but they are additive to a foundation built on outcome. A seller whose home sold for the right price in the right time will forgive small communication failures. A seller whose home didn't sell will not be saved by excellent communication.

The implication: the way to maximize seller satisfaction is mostly to do an excellent job of Goal 1, with a thin layer of communication discipline on top. Not the other way around.

3. Self-promotion — a byproduct, not a strategy

Self-promotion that comes from doing Goal 1 well is enormously powerful. When a property sells faster than expected at a great price because of distinctive marketing, that's a story worth telling, listings to show, sellers who refer. The promotion writes itself, grounded in real results.

Self-promotion pursued as its own goal during a live listing is dangerous. It centers the agent instead of the property. It teaches sellers, slowly and unconsciously, that their agent is more interested in being seen than in selling their home. It also gets attention from the wrong audience — generic followers rather than future clients and buyer agents.

The principle: promote the work; don't promote yourself.

The Operating Test

Does this primarily move the property toward sale?

If yes — do it. Satisfaction and promotion follow as byproducts.

If no, but it makes the seller feel cared for in ways property work doesn't directly produce — do it judiciously, with discipline, in service of the relationship.

If no, and it primarily exists to make the agent look like they're working — don't do it. It's theater. The cost is real (time, the seller's calibration, professional reputation among buyer agents) and the benefit is illusory.

Part Two

The Four Tiers — A Test for Every Tactic

Categorizing marketing activity by how much real work it does

Every potential marketing activity falls into one of four tiers. Knowing which tier a tactic belongs to tells you how much of your time and budget it deserves.

Tier 1 Real Marketing That's Also Visible

These do the actual work of producing buyers and the seller can see them happening. Best of both worlds. Most of the listing's time and energy belongs here.

  • Direct outreach to qualified buyer agents. Identify agents whose recent activity suggests likely-fit clients. Reach out before listing goes live: "I'm bringing a property to market next week that I think one of your clients might want to see before it's public."
  • Online performance tracking with strategic interpretation. Compile MLS portal views, saves, inquiries weekly. Interpret what they mean. The interpretation is the value-add.
  • Targeted neighborhood outreach. Printed "just listed" pieces, hand-delivered to immediate streets. Some sales come from neighbors referring friends and family.
  • Strategic open houses with pre-event invitations. Email specific buyer agents and likely-fit prospects in advance. Dramatically more qualified attendance than generic open houses.
  • Showing feedback synthesis. Follow up with every buyer agent after a showing. Compile patterns weekly. "The pattern across four showings is that buyers love the kitchen but feel the master is small." That synthesis is real intelligence; it informs adjustments.

Tier 2 Visibility That's Also Genuinely Useful

These do less heavy lifting on producing offers, but they meaningfully expand reach and they are visible to sellers.

  • Social media videos. Walk-throughs, lifestyle vignettes, neighborhood tours. Reach buyer audiences MLS doesn't. Worth doing if quality can be maintained without consuming all available time.
  • Curated photo updates. A blooming tree, seasonal change, different light. "I noticed the back garden is in full bloom this week — refreshed the listing photos."
  • Listing description refreshes. Slight rewording in week three signals freshness to portals and incorporates showing feedback.
  • Featured / boosted placement. Selectively, perhaps in week three after the initial-launch surge subsides. Once or twice — not constantly.
  • Print collateral. Property feature sheets, neighborhood brochures. Few direct buyers; signals seriousness to buyer agents.

Tier 3 Visibility-Forward, Useful in Moderation

These do less actual marketing work, but they reassure sellers and have small marginal value.

  • Weekly written status updates — even when nothing has changed. Silence is interpreted as inactivity.
  • Pre-showing texts to the seller. Each showing becomes an event the seller can see being managed.
  • Agent broker tours / luncheons. Rarely produce direct buyers but build awareness and give something specific to report.

Tier 4 Tactics to Use Sparingly or Avoid

These often don't help the listing and sometimes actively dilute the marketing.

  • High-volume social media posting. One thoughtful video per week is marketing. Five rushed posts per week is noise — and signals to buyer agents that the agent is more interested in being seen than in selling the home.
  • Repeated price-drop announcements. Each one signals weakness. If a price reduction is needed, do it once, deliberately, with strategy.
  • Constant boosting / featuring. Diminishing returns. Algorithms learn to deprioritize.
  • Generic paid lead-generation ads. Produce general buyer prospects who rarely match the specific listing.
Part Three

Phase One — Pre-MLS (the most underused window)

The 7-14 days between signing and going live are where great agents separate from average ones

Most agents treat the pre-MLS window as logistics — photos, sign installation, MLS data entry. Great agents treat it as the highest-leverage marketing window of the entire listing, because every action taken before launch compounds at launch.

The seller experiences this phase as "my agent is preparing seriously." That impression is what builds trust before the property has had a chance to perform. It's also when the most consequential strategic decisions get locked in.

Pre-MLS Tasks

Tier 1 · Day 1–3
Strategic Listing Plan Document

Within 48 hours of signing, send the seller a brief written document outlining: target buyer profile, pricing rationale with comps, marketing timeline, photography plan, scheduled intervention points (week 2, week 4, week 6), and what success looks like. This document is the seller's anchor for the entire listing period — they refer back to it every time they wonder "what's the plan?"

Tier 1 · Day 1–5
Pre-Photography Property Prep

Walk through the home with the seller, room by room (using the Listing Tour Script). Identify what stays, what gets rearranged, what gets removed. Recommend professional staging consultation if needed. The seller sees the work being thought through, not improvised.

Tier 1 · Day 5–8
Professional Photography (Timed to the Light)

Book the photographer for the time of day the seller said the home looks best (per the tour question about light). Most agents shoot at the photographer's convenience. Shooting at the home's best moment is one of the most underrated competitive advantages in residential marketing.

Tier 1 · Day 7–10
Pre-Launch Buyer Agent Outreach

Identify 8–15 buyer agents whose recent activity suggests likely-fit clients (via MLS searches, recent comparable showings, neighborhood relationships). Reach out individually before launch: "I'm bringing a property to market on [date] that I think one of your clients might want to see. Happy to give you and them a preview look." This is the single most underused tactic in residential real estate.

Tier 2 · Day 8–12
Social Media Content Pre-Built

Produce one walk-through video, one lifestyle/exterior video, one neighborhood vignette. Have all three ready before launch — schedule them across week 1, 3, and 5 of the active period. Producing content during the listing under deadline pressure tends to produce worse content than producing it ahead.

Tier 1 · Day 10–12
Listing Description and Feature Sheet Drafted

Write the listing description using the seller's own words from the tour. Lead with what the seller said made them say yes to the house. The description that captures emotional truth produces showings; the description that lists features produces drive-bys.

Tier 1 · Day 12–14
Final Strategic Walkthrough with Seller

Before launch, walk through the strategic plan once more with the seller. Confirm price, timeline, marketing rhythm, expected showing pace, your weekly update cadence. This is where you set expectations explicitly so the seller has a frame for everything that follows.

Pre-MLS Checklist
  • Strategic Listing Plan document delivered to seller
  • Property prep walkthrough completed
  • Professional photography scheduled at the home's best light
  • Pre-launch buyer agent outreach (8–15 contacts) completed and documented
  • Social media content produced and scheduled (3 pieces minimum)
  • Listing description and feature sheet drafted, leading with seller's own language
  • Floor plans, professional photography, virtual tour finalized
  • Sign installed, lockbox set
  • Final pre-launch strategic walkthrough with seller completed
  • Launch day expectations set: when, what to expect, how communication will work
Part Four

Phase Two — Active Listing, Week by Week

A disciplined rhythm that produces real activity and visible work

The principle of the active phase: don't perform constant activity — build a rhythm and let the rhythm carry the visibility. Each week has a baseline routine; certain weeks have scheduled interventions. The seller experiences this as a professional running a marketing program, not an agent improvising.

The Weekly Baseline (Every Week of Active Listing)

Regardless of week number, every active listing receives this baseline rhythm:

  • Monday — Online performance check. Pull view counts, saves, inquiries from MLS portals. Note trends.
  • Monday/Tuesday — Buyer-agent outreach round. 3–5 fresh contacts to agents whose recent activity suggests fit.
  • Throughout the week — Showings happen. Pre-showing text to seller before each. Feedback collected from buyer agents within 24 hours after.
  • Thursday — Showing feedback synthesis written up. Patterns identified.
  • Friday — Weekly seller update sent. Includes metrics, synthesis, what was done this week, what's planned next week. (See Part Six for format.)

The Friday update is the visibility mechanism. The rest of the week is the actual work. Both are non-negotiable.

Week One

Launch Surge

Maximum visibility, qualified showings

The listing goes live. The first 7 days produce the largest natural surge of attention — most online inquiries, most showings, most buyer-agent interest. The job is to capture and convert that surge.

Tier 1 · Launch Day
Live MLS launch + buyer-agent push

Simultaneously: MLS goes live, social media post #1 publishes, individual emails go to the 8–15 buyer agents pre-contacted. The buyer agents now know the listing has gone live and have a one-click path to book a showing.

Tier 1 · Days 2–4
First showings + immediate feedback collection

Most showings in the first week come from the launch surge. Follow up with every buyer agent within 24 hours of the showing. Even a brief "What was your client's reaction?" via text produces useful intelligence.

Tier 1 · Day 5–6
First open house

Pre-event email invitations sent to specific buyer agents and likely-fit prospects. Hand-delivered flyers on the immediate streets. Sign placement strategic. Track attendance and follow up within 48 hours.

Friday Update Includes

Online stats from the launch week. Number of showings booked vs held. Initial buyer-agent feedback themes. Open house attendance. Your interpretation of what the data tells us about market reception. Plan for week 2.

Week Two

First Calibration

Read the data, refine the message

The launch surge has run its course. Week 2 is when the underlying market signal becomes visible — and when the first strategic intervention happens.

Tier 1 · Throughout
Showing feedback synthesis becomes meaningful

By week 2, you have 5–10 showings of feedback. Patterns are visible. Common positives, common concerns. Write up the synthesis in plain language. "Buyers love the kitchen and the natural light. Three buyers mentioned the master bedroom feels small. One buyer was concerned about the basement ceiling height."

Tier 2 · Mid-Week
Scheduled Intervention #1: Description Refresh

Based on showing feedback, refine the listing description. Lead with whatever buyers responded most strongly to. Address subtly any common concern (without naming it as an issue). This refresh signals to portals that the listing is fresh; it also incorporates real intelligence.

Tier 2 · Mid-Week
Social media post #2 — lifestyle / neighborhood

The video produced pre-launch publishes this week. Different angle than the walk-through (which led week 1). Captures the way of living the home offers, not just the property.

Tier 1 · Friday/Weekend
Mid-Process Service Feedback Check-in

Two weeks in is the right moment to ask the seller: "Is the rhythm of our communication working for you? Anything I could be doing differently?" This is the asking practice from the Service Procedures playbook. Capture in the tracker.

Friday Update Includes

Cumulative showing count and recent feedback synthesis. Online performance comparison week 1 vs week 2. The description refresh. Strategic read: is the listing performing on track, ahead, or behind expectations? Plan for week 3.

Week Three

Re-energize

If no offer yet, deliberate refresh

By week 3, if no offer is in hand, attention naturally cools. This is when most agents go quiet. This is when the disciplined agent gets louder — strategically.

Tier 2 · Early Week
Scheduled Intervention #2: Photo refresh

If conditions allow, fresh photos. New season, different light, different staging detail. Even one or two new photos signals freshness and gives social media something new to share.

Tier 1 · Mid-Week
Second wave of buyer-agent outreach

Different cohort than the pre-launch round. Agents whose buyer activity has picked up since launch. "Wanted to make sure you'd seen [property] — three weeks in, still showing well, and I think your buyer profile is a good fit. Happy to arrange a private showing."

Tier 2 · Mid-Week
Social media post #3 — dwelling on a specific feature

The third pre-produced video publishes. Often a single-feature focus: the kitchen, the backyard, the natural light. Reaches buyers who scrolled past the broader videos.

Tier 2 · End of Week
Featured / boosted placement on portals

Selective paid promotion. One time. Drives a fresh wave of online attention.

Friday Update Includes

Three-week performance summary. The interventions of this week (refresh, outreach round, boosted placement). Showing feedback patterns. If the listing is performing below expectations, this is the moment to begin gently introducing the price-conversation framework — bring three options, the agent's recommendation, the seller's decision.

Week Four

Strategic Decision Point

Hold, adjust, or change strategy

Four weeks of market data is enough to make a real strategic decision. Week 4 is when the conversation about hold, adjust, or change strategy happens explicitly with the seller — using the System-Based Delivery framework.

Tier 1 · Beginning of Week
Strategic Options Document

Bring three options to the seller in writing: (1) hold the current price and continue the marketing program; (2) adjust pricing by a specific amount with rationale; (3) change marketing strategy (different photographer, restage, repositioning). Include the data supporting each option, the trade-offs, and your recommendation.

Tier 1 · Mid-Week
Strategic conversation with seller

Walk through the document together — preferably in person or on a video call. Use the Validate / Draw the Line / Hand Them the Map structure. The seller chooses; you execute.

Tier 1 · End of Week
Execute the chosen path

Whatever the seller decides, the next week starts with concrete action. If price adjustment: re-list with adjusted price, new round of buyer-agent outreach announcing the change. If hold: re-energize with new tactic of choice. If strategy change: implement immediately.

Friday Update Includes

The strategic options document (sent before the meeting, not at it). After-meeting written confirmation of the decision and the plan for week 5. This document becomes part of the seller's record — they can refer back to it as evidence of how the decision was made.

Week Five and Beyond

Sustained Discipline

Same rhythm; new interventions

Once past the strategic decision point, the listing settles into one of two trajectories: (1) the chosen path produces an offer in 1–3 weeks; (2) further calibration is required.

The weekly baseline (Monday performance check, Tuesday outreach, Friday update) continues unchanged. Scheduled interventions space out: photo or staging change at week 6 if needed; second strategic options document at week 8 if no offer.

Tier 1 · Week 6
Scheduled Intervention #3 (if needed)

Either a refresh (new photos, restage, description rewrite) or a further strategic conversation. The pre-decided rhythm prevents the listing from going stale and prevents the agent from running out of things to do.

Tier 1 · Week 8
Second Strategic Options Document (if no offer)

If the listing is past 8 weeks without an offer, a second formal options conversation is required. The same Validate / Draw the Line / Hand Them the Map structure. Possibly: substantial price adjustment, withdraw and re-launch with different angle, or take the property off the market until conditions improve.

Tier 1 · Throughout
Maintain the weekly Friday update — without exception

Even when nothing has changed. "Quiet week — two showings booked for next weekend. Online views holding steady. Reaching out to three more buyer agents this Tuesday. Will have feedback for you Friday." Silence at this stage is the most damaging communication failure possible.

Part Five

Phase Three — Conditional, Firm, and Closing

Marketing-related work continues through closing — just differently

Once an offer is accepted, the marketing job changes shape. The work shifts from attracting buyers to protecting the deal and the seller's experience. But the rhythm of visible work continues.

Conditional Period

Tier 1
Daily-or-near-daily check-ins with the buyer agent

Track the financing condition, inspection condition, status condition. Brief written updates to the seller every 2–3 days even if nothing has changed: "Financing condition still pending — buyer's agent confirmed lender is reviewing today. Inspection booked for Wednesday." The seller's anxiety during conditional periods is enormous; visibility into progress is the antidote.

Tier 2
Maintain the listing's online presence (briefly)

Don't pull marketing immediately. Conditions can fall through. Keep the listing visible for the duration of the conditional period — though signal accordingly (status shows "conditional" on MLS).

Tier 1
After Major Milestone Service Feedback Check-in

Once the deal is firm, ask the seller: "Looking back at how I've handled this so far, is there anything you'd want me to do differently as we go into closing?" The asking practice from the Service Procedures playbook. Capture in the tracker.

Firm Period (Sold Firm to Closing Day)

Tier 1
Marketing artifacts archived for the post-sale story

Save copies of all marketing produced during the listing — videos, photos, descriptions, performance metrics, the strategic options documents. These become the raw material for the post-sale debrief and any future case-study work.

Tier 1
Weekly closing-period status check

Even when nothing has changed: "Quick update — financing waived, on track for closing on [date]." The pre-closing weeks are when sellers often experience the highest anxiety; weekly written confirmation is essential.

Tier 1
Pre-closing logistics document

One week before closing, send the seller a written document outlining: walkthrough timing, key handover plan, final utility readings, lawyer coordination, what to bring, what to expect. Eliminates 80% of last-minute questions.

Part Six

Phase Four — Post-Sale Debrief and Earned Promotion

Where Goal 3 finally becomes appropriate to pursue directly

The post-sale phase is where self-promotion stops being inappropriate and starts being earned. The work has been done; the result is in. Now the story can be told — grounded in actual results, not asserted ahead of them.

Debrief Tasks

Tier 1 · Day 1–7 Post-Close
Post-Close Service Feedback Debrief

The most valuable feedback moment in the entire relationship. Ask honestly: "What worked well in how I served you? And — honestly — what could have been better? I ask every client this. The only way I improve is if people are willing to tell me." Capture exact words in the tracker. This produces the most actionable improvement data possible.

Tier 1 · Day 7–14 Post-Close
Internal Listing Debrief Document

Write a short internal document covering: launch metrics, what worked, what didn't, time-to-offer, sale-to-list ratio, key lessons. This becomes the case-study material for future presentations and the institutional memory for refining the procedure.

Tier 3 · Day 14–30 Post-Close
Earned promotional content (with permission)

If the listing performed well, request permission from the seller to share the story. Frame it as the marketing strategy, not the agent. "Listed at $X, sold at $Y in Z days — here's what worked: pre-launch buyer outreach, photography timed to the home's best light, weekly performance interpretation." The story is about the system, not the storyteller.

Tier 1 · Day 30 Post-Close
30-day check-in with the seller

The settled, post-stress moment. Ideal moment to ask for a referral — framed around the business model: "The part of this work I value most is referrals from clients I've served well."

Tier 1 · 6-month and 1-year
Ongoing relationship maintenance

Light, non-transactional check-ins. Birthday and important-date acknowledgment. Holiday note. Most agents disappear after closing; the agents who don't are the ones who get called when the seller's friend, neighbor, or family member needs a real estate referral.

The Self-Promotion Principle

Promote the work, not the worker

The agent who wins long-term in the GTA market wins by being so good at the substance that the promotion takes care of itself. Lean hard into self-promotion early and you peak quickly, then plateau. Build the substance. Tell the story of the substance afterward. Let the promotion be the shadow that the substance casts.

Part Seven

The Visibility Discipline — Weekly Seller Update Format

The single artifact that turns invisible work into visible service

The Friday weekly update is the most important communication artifact of the entire active listing period. It is the mechanism by which all the work being done — the buyer-agent outreach, the performance tracking, the showing feedback synthesis, the strategic thinking — becomes visible to the seller.

It needs structure. Same structure every week. The seller gets used to it; the rhythm itself becomes reassuring.

The Five Sections of Every Friday Update

1. The Numbers

Showings this week / total. Online views, saves, inquiries (week-over-week). Open house attendance if applicable. Count of buyer-agent outreach contacts made.

2. What Buyers Are Saying

Synthesized feedback from showings. Patterns, not raw quotes. "Buyers consistently praise the kitchen and natural light. Two of four mentioned the master feels smaller than they expected; one was concerned about the basement ceiling height."

3. What I Did This Week

Specific. "Reached out to 5 buyer agents whose recent activity matches your home. Refreshed two photos to capture the spring blooms in the back garden. Submitted listing for featured placement on Realtor.ca through next Tuesday."

4. My Read of the Data

The interpretation. "View counts are strong but conversion to inquiries has slowed in week 3 — this is normal pattern. The feedback theme on the master suggests buyers in our target range are looking for either more square footage or a stronger price-to-comp positioning. We're approaching the moment when a strategic options conversation makes sense."

5. Next Week's Plan

Specific actions. "Tuesday: another round of buyer-agent outreach (different cohort). Wednesday: refreshed listing description goes live. Saturday: open house with pre-event email invitations. Friday: I'll send the next update with showing feedback and strategic recommendation."
